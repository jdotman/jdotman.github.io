The basin walls are coated in mineral deposits that have built up into smooth terraces of orange and white over many centuries.
Perfumery chambers near the private quarters hold copper stills and glass vessels designed to extract fragrant oils from flowers and herbs grown in the gardens. The stills are connected by thin tubes of hammered metal and the collection flasks at each end are stained amber from the oils that passed through them. Dried petals fill baskets beneath each station and the scent that clings to the walls is faint but still recognizable after all the passing years.
Barrel vaults in the cellar are built from wedge shaped stones fitted without mortar and the engineering distributes the weight of the building above perfectly. Each vault spans the full width of the room and the keystones at the apex are carved with the mason marks of the builders who set them. The vaults have not shifted in centuries and the joins are still tight enough that water does not penetrate from above.
Framed maps on the walls of the command room show the surrounding terrain at different scales and the largest covers the wall from floor to ceiling. Colored pins mark strategic positions and the legend on the desk explains each color though the dispositions they represent are centuries out of date. One pin sits at the center of the map marking this fortress and someone has drawn a circle around it in red ink.
Passage walls are embedded with shells and the remains of marine creatures from an era when this stone formed the floor of a shallow tropical sea. The fossils are so well preserved that individual spines and teeth are visible in the cross sections exposed by the miners who cut this tunnel. Walking here is walking through the compressed record of an ocean that vanished before any creature on land had learned to write.
Beekeeper stations along the south wall hold rows of clay hive vessels arranged on stone shelves angled to catch the afternoon sun through gaps in the masonry. The bees are long gone but the comb inside the vessels has hardened into a dark amber mass that still smells faintly of old honey and warm wax. Smoker bellows hang from hooks nearby and the leather has dried and cracked but the metal cups are intact and blackened from many seasons of use.
Winding paths through the cave system follow the flow of an underground river that has carved its channel deeper into the rock over geological time. The river is audible before it is visible and when you reach the bank the water runs dark and fast over a bed of polished stones. Bridges of natural stone span the channel where harder rock resisted erosion and each crossing requires careful judgment of the ancient stone.
